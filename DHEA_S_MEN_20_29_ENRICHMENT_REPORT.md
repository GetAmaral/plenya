### Reference Ranges by Age
- 20-24 years: 226-361 μg/dL (mean 286 μg/dL)
- 25-29 years: 200-350 μg/dL (broad lab range: 110-510 μg/dL)
- Conversion formula: μg/dL × 0.027 = μmol/L

### Clinical Interpretation

**LOW LEVELS (<110 μg/dL):**
1. Investigate primary or secondary adrenal insufficiency
2. Order: baseline cortisol 8am, ACTH, electrolytes (Na+, K+), glucose
3. If cortisol <10 μg/dL + low DHEA-S: ~72% probability of adrenal insufficiency
4. Consider ACTH stimulation test if diagnosis uncertain
5. Assess recent glucocorticoid use (reduces DHEA-S diagnostic accuracy)
6. Rule out: Addison's disease, hypopituitarism, iatrogenic suppression
7. Associated symptoms: profound fatigue, hypotension, skin hyperpigmentation

**NORMAL LEVELS (110-510 μg/dL):**
1. Adequate adrenal function
2. Establish individual baseline for future monitoring
3. Reassess annually if family history of adrenal diseases
4. Correlate with total and free testosterone for complete androgenic assessment

**ELEVATED LEVELS (>510 μg/dL):**
1. Investigate congenital adrenal hyperplasia (21-hydroxylase deficiency)
2. Rule out androgen-producing tumor (adrenal or testicular)
3. Assess Cushing's syndrome (order 24-hour urinary free cortisol)
4. Order: 17-OH-progesterone, androstenedione, total testosterone
5. Consider adrenal imaging (CT or MRI) if marked elevation (>2x upper limit)
6. Assess signs of virilization or metabolic syndrome

### Age-Specific Considerations (20-29 Years)
- Low DHEA-S at this age is ALWAYS pathological (not age-related)
- DHEA-S + baseline cortisol combination has high negative predictive value
- If both normal: <1.2% probability of adrenal insufficiency
- Consider genetic factors: 37-46% of variability is hereditary
- Assess clinical context: chronic stress, extreme exercise, malnutrition can reduce levels
- Monitor trends: decline >5% per year may indicate underlying pathology

### Follow-Up Protocol
- Normal values + asymptomatic: reassess in 1-2 years
- Borderline values: repeat in 3-6 months with simultaneous morning cortisol
- Abnormal values: refer to endocrinologist for comprehensive investigation
- Document medications in use (glucocorticoids, anabolic steroids, opioids)

### Important Correlations
- DHEA-S contributes ~30% of total androgens in men
- Assess with testosterone for complete androgenic status
- Low DHEA-S + normal testosterone: suggests isolated adrenal problem
- Both low: consider hypogonadotropic hypogonadism or secondary adrenal insufficiency
